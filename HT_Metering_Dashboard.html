≤ 1500 KVA

HTCT metering at LT side (415V). CT/PT installed at consumer end. Standard 3-phase 3-wire or 4-wire configuration.

1501 – 5000 KVA

> 5001 KVA

EHV metering. ABT metering mandatory. CVT (Capacitor Voltage Transformer) replaces electromagnetic PT. Traction feeders included.

Tariff Structure (Two-Part Tariff)

Demand Charge

Maximum Demand (MD)

  • Recorded by Trivector / TOD meter
  • Measured in KVA (apparent power)
  • Peak 30-min demand in billing period
  • Billed even if MD not reached (minimum billing = 75% contracted demand)
  • Power factor incentive/penalty applies
Energy Charge

Energy Consumption

  • KWH — Active energy (real power)
  • KVARH — Reactive energy
  • KVAH = √(KWH² + KVARH²)
  • TOD meters record time-of-day slots
  • ABT — Deviation charge based on schedule

Metering Types

Classification by metering scheme and billing function

LTCT Metering

Low Tension CT Metering

Meter installed at LT (415V) side. CTs installed in LT panel. PT not required — direct voltage connection to 415V bus.

  • CT ratio typically 100/5A or 200/5A
  • Direct voltage connection 240V / 415V
  • 3-phase 4-wire balanced/unbalanced
  • Used for 11kV consumers with LT board metering
  • Multiplying Factor (MF) = CT ratio
HTCT Metering

High Tension CT Metering

Meter at HT side. Both CT and PT required. 3-phase 3-wire for 11kV/33kV systems.

  • CT: 50/5A, 100/1A, or as designed
  • PT: 11000/110V or 33000/110V
  • MF = CT ratio × PT ratio
  • Accuracy class 0.5S or 0.2S mandatory
  • Separate metering CT cores from protection cores
TOD Metering

Time-of-Day Metering

Records energy consumption in predefined time slots. Peak / off-peak / night tariff zones.

  • Inbuilt Real Time Clock (RTC)
  • Typically 6 time slots programmable
  • Records KWH, KVARH, KVAH per slot
  • Load survey / load profile storage
  • Optical port / RS485 communication
ABT Metering

Availability Based Tariff

CERC mandate for bulk consumers ≥1MW and all generators. Frequency-linked UI (Unscheduled Interchange) charges.

  • Main meter + Check meter mandatory
  • Standby meter optional (recommended)
  • Records energy every 15 minutes
  • UI (deviation) accounted separately
  • CT class 0.2S, PT class 0.2 mandatory
  • Sealed by STU (State Transmission Utility)
Trivector Meter

Trivector / MD Meter

Measures three quantities: KW (active), KVAR (reactive), KVA (apparent). Records maximum demand.

  • Thermal demand indicator (30-min integration)
  • KWH, KVARH, KVAH registration
  • Power factor = KWH / KVAH
  • MD printed on demand slip
  • Used for two-part tariff billing
Whole Current

Whole Current Meter (LT Direct)

No CT or PT. Current flows directly through meter current coils. Used for small LT consumers.

  • Rating: 5A, 10A, 20A, 40A, 60A
  • Single phase or 3-phase 4-wire
  • No MF required (MF = 1)
  • Class 1 or Class 2 accuracy
  • Direct connection up to 100A

Energy Registration Quantities

Active Energy (KWH)

P = V·I·cos φ [Watts]
KWH = Σ P·dt

Real power doing useful work. Drives KWH disc forward on induction type meters.

Reactive Energy (KVARH)

Q = V·I·sin φ [VAR]
KVARH = Σ Q·dt

Magnetising current component. High KVAR = low power factor = penalty charges.

Apparent Energy (KVAH)

KVAH = √(KWH² + KVARH²)

Total energy drawn from system. ABT billing increasingly on KVAH basis.

132 / 220 kV EHV System — CVT & Traction▼

Capacitor Voltage Transformer (CVT)

Electromagnetic PT impractical above 33kV due to insulation cost. CVT uses capacitor divider + electromagnetic unit.

  • Primary: capacitor stack (C1, C2) in series
  • Intermediate voltage extracted at junction (~5–15kV)
  • Electromagnetic unit steps down to 110V
  • Burden: 25–100 VA typical
  • Ferroresonance suppression damper required
  • Also used for PLC (Power Line Carrier) coupling

CT Burden Calculation & Knee Point Voltage▼

Burden Calculation

Burden (VA) = I²sec × R_total
R_total = R_CT_secondary + R_lead + R_meter

At 5A secondary:
Burden = 25 × R_total (VA)
  • Lead resistance: ρL/A (copper, 2-way)
  • Meter burden: typically 0.5–2 VA
  • Total must be ≤ rated CT burden
  • 1A secondary → less lead loss, preferred for long runs

Knee Point Voltage (Kpv)

Kpv ≥ ALF × I_rated × (RCT + Rburden)

For 5P20 CT, ALF=20:
Kpv ≥ 20 × 5 × (RCT + Rburden)
Example: RCT=1Ω, Rb=1Ω → Kpv ≥ 200V
Polarity Test: Battery + → P1 (primary), Galvanometer deflects forward at S1 (secondary). P1-P2 primary; S1-S2 secondary. Dot convention: current IN at P1, current OUT at S1.

CT, PT, meters, test blocks, testing kits — specifications and construction

Current Transformer (CT) — Construction & Types▼
Bar Type CT

Single-turn primary (busbar passes through). Used for very high currents. Simple construction, high ratio.

Wound Type CT

Multi-turn primary winding. Used for lower ratios (e.g., 50/5A). Better accuracy at low currents.

Ring Type CT

Toroidal core. Cable passes through. Used for earth fault detection (core balance / Holmgreen). Zero-sequence detection.

Multi-Core CT Construction

  • Core 1: Metering (class 0.2S or 0.5S, low burden)
  • Core 2: Protection (class 5P10 or 5P20, high Kpv)
  • Core 3: Differential protection (PS class, low Rct)
  • Primary: shared for all cores
  • Each core has independent secondary terminals
NEVER open circuit a CT secondary while primary carries current. High voltage develops across open secondary — potentially lethal and destructive.

Potential Transformer (PT/VT) — Types & Specifications▼

3-Limb vs 5-Limb Core

  • 3-Limb: Cannot carry zero-sequence flux (no return path). Used where earth fault voltage measurement not needed.
  • 5-Limb: Two outer limbs provide zero-sequence flux path. Required for earth fault (residual voltage) detection via open-delta winding.
  • Open delta winding: 3rd winding in open-delta. Healthy condition: output ≈ 0V. Earth fault: output rises to ≈ 110V (residual).
PT Ratio = V_primary / V_secondary
11kV PT: 11000/√3 : 110/√3 = 100:1 per phase
Or L-L: 11000:110 = 100:1

Test Terminal Block (TTB) — Test Block Operation▼

TTB (Test Terminal Block) enables meter testing without disconnecting CT/PT circuits. Has three terminal types:

M — Metering

Connects to meter terminals. Isolating links allow meter to be isolated from circuit for testing.

A — Ammeter

CT secondary circuit passes through. Short-circuit switch allows CT secondary to be shorted before opening meter circuit.

L — Link

Voltage circuit links. Voltage can be isolated from meter without interrupting PT secondary circuit.

TTB Operation Procedure (Meter Replacement)

1

Short CT Secondaries

Use shorting screws on TTB 'A' terminals. Short all phase CT secondaries before any disconnection.

2

Isolate Voltage Links

Remove 'L' (link) connections on TTB. Voltage now isolated from meter safely.

3

Open Meter Links

Open 'M' isolating links on TTB. Meter now fully isolated — can be replaced.

4

Replace Meter / Test

Connect test meter or new meter. Apply test currents/voltages via TTB test terminals.

5

Restore in Reverse

Close M links → restore L voltage links → remove CT shorts. Verify polarity before restoring.

Safety: Never remove CT shorts before closing M links. Never open CT secondary without shorting first.
Meter Sealing — 4 Sealing Points▼
  • Seal Point 1: Top cover seal — prevents access to meter internals (disc, magnets, shunt)
  • Seal Point 2: Inspection cover seal — access flap for reading without seal breaking
  • Seal Point 3: Secondary terminal box seal — CT/PT secondary connections inside
  • Seal Point 4: Cable entry seal — gland/conduit entry to meter enclosure
Who Seals:
DISCOM AE/ADE seals after installation verification. STU/SLDC seals ABT meters. Meter must be sealed before commissioning. Broken seal = prima facie evidence of tampering.
  • Sealing wire: lead seal with authority stamp
  • Seal register maintained at SDOA/division office
  • Meter box external lock also required
Summation CTs & Z-Connection▼

Summation CT

Combines currents from multiple feeders into single meter. Used where feeder summation required (e.g., single billing point for multiple transformers).

  • Multiple primaries wound on single toroid core
  • Each primary = individual feeder CT secondary
  • Output (secondary) feeds single energy meter
  • Ratio = sum of individual turns
  • Accuracy affected by phase diversity between feeders
FACOR single-point metering arrangement uses summation CTs for multiple transformer feeders feeding a common bus.

Z-Connection of CTs

Used to obtain 3-phase 4-wire metering from 3-wire CT installation. Provides artificial neutral using CT secondaries.

Z-connection: Two CTs (R and B phases)
I_Y = -(I_R + I_B) by Kirchhoff's law
Third element current derived, not measured
Used when only 2 CTs available in 3-wire system
  • Reduces CT count from 3 to 2
  • Acceptable for balanced/near-balanced loads
  • Not suitable for unbalanced industrial loads
  • Also used in some 3-element meter arrangements

Terminal Configurations

18 standard terminal configurations for energy meters (IS 14697 / CEA)

18 Terminal Configurations cover all combinations of: AC current (direct/CT), AC voltage (direct/PT), active/reactive power, single-phase/3-phase-3-wire/3-phase-4-wire balanced/unbalanced, power factor, and frequency.

AC Current Measurement

Config 1

AC Current — Direct

Current flows directly through meter current terminals. Single phase. No CT. Rating: 5A, 10A, 20A, 40A.

Config 2

AC Current — Transformer Connected

Current via CT. Secondary (5A or 1A) to meter current coil. Polarity: P1→S1. MF = CT ratio.

AC Voltage Measurement

Config 3

AC Voltage — Direct

LT direct connection to voltage coil. 240V (phase-neutral) or 415V (phase-phase). No PT required.

Config 4

AC Voltage — Transformer Connected

Via PT. Secondary 110V (phase-phase) or 63.5V (phase-neutral) to meter voltage coil. MF includes PT ratio.

Active Power (KWH) Configurations

Config 5

Active Power — 1-Phase (Direct)

P = V·I·cos φ. Single element meter. Direct current and voltage connections.

Config 6

Active Power — 1-Phase (CT+PT)

As Config 5 but via instrument transformers. MF = CT ratio × PT ratio.

Config 7

Active Power — 3φ 3-Wire (2 Element)

2-wattmeter method. Uses R-phase and B-phase elements. Y-phase CT not required. Accurate for all loads.

P = V_RY·I_R·cosφ_RY + V_BY·I_B·cosφ_BY
Config 8

Active Power — 3φ 3-Wire (3 Element)

3 elements, 3 CTs. More accurate for unbalanced systems. Summation gives total active power.

Config 9

Active Power — 3φ 4-Wire Balanced

1 element × 3 = single element with neutral return. Only valid for perfectly balanced 3-phase loads.

Config 10

Active Power — 3φ 4-Wire Unbalanced

3-element meter with neutral. R+Y+B elements independently measure each phase. Required for unbalanced LT loads.

Reactive Power (KVARH) Configurations

Config 11

Reactive Power — 1-Phase

Q = V·I·sin φ. Voltage circuit shifted 90° internally. Phase-shifted current element drives disc in lag direction.

Config 12

Reactive Power — 3φ 3-Wire

90° phase shift method. Cross-connection of voltages to current elements. Standard for 3-wire systems.

Config 13

Reactive Power — 3φ 4-Wire Balanced

Single element reactive meter for balanced LT system. Not suitable for unbalanced loads.

Config 14

Reactive Power — 3φ 4-Wire Unbalanced

3-element reactive with neutral. Each phase independently. Required for mixed single-phase/3-phase loads.

Derived Quantities

Config 15

Active + Reactive — Combined

Trivector meter. Simultaneously records KWH and KVARH. Modern static meters compute KVAH internally.

Config 16

Power Factor Meter

Measures cos φ directly. Crossed-coil moving element. Indicates unity at centre scale. Lagging/leading graduation.

Config 17

Frequency Meter

Vibrating reed or resonance type. Connected to voltage bus. Reads 50 Hz nominal. Used in grid frequency monitoring (ABT).

Config 18

Maximum Demand Indicator

Thermal or electronic integration over 30-min period. Pointer held at peak; reset monthly by DISCOM staff.

Selector Switch Connections

Ammeter Selector Switch

5-position rotary switch: OFF, R, Y, B, N. Routes selected phase CT secondary to panel ammeter.

Switch has make-before-break contacts to ensure CT secondary never open-circuited during phase selection.

Voltmeter Selector Switch

7-position rotary: OFF, RY, YB, BR, RN, YN, BN. Routes selected voltage to panel voltmeter.

Break-before-make acceptable here — no danger from open PT secondary. High impedance voltmeter load.

Energy Registration Fault Analysis

28 cases — 12 for 3-phase 3-wire, 16 for 3-phase 4-wire meters

Reference: KSP Kumar manuscript, pages 43–51. All cases based on CT/PT fuse missing, polarity reversal, or lead interchange. Registration effect calculated from phasor analysis.
3-Phase 3-Wire Meter — 12 Fault Cases▼

Normal registration for 3φ 3-wire 2-element meter = 2·V·I·cos φ (unity PF = full registration). Effect shown as fraction of correct reading.

Case 1 — One CT Secondary Open (No current R-phase)

Registration = 50% of correct (only 1 of 2 elements active). Meter under-reads by half.

Case 2 — One CT Polarity Reversed (R-phase reversed)

Element 1 drives reverse. Net registration = zero or negative depending on PF. At unity PF: registration = 0. Meter stops or reverses.

Case 3 — Both CTs Polarity Reversed

Both elements reverse. Disc rotates backward. Meter reverses — energy credit to consumer (theft scenario). Registration = –100%.

Case 4 — One PT Fuse Missing (R-phase voltage missing)

Element 1 loses voltage reference. Only Element 2 (B-Y) registers. Registration = 50% of correct approximately.

Case 5 — R & Y Phase Current Leads Interchanged

Elements see wrong phase current. Registration = cos(60°–φ) + cos(60°+φ) = cos60·cosφ only. At unity PF: ~50% registration.

Case 6 — R & B Phase Current Leads Interchanged

Phasor analysis: net registration distorted. At unity PF = 86.6% (cos30°). Depends on load PF angle.

Case 7 — Y & B Voltage Leads Interchanged

Voltage phasors rotated. For standard 2-element connection: registration = cos(120°–φ) + cos(φ). At unity PF ≈ 50%.

Case 8 — R Phase CT Missing (only B-phase CT in circuit)

Only one element functional. Registration = 50% approx. Actual depends on load balance.

Case 9 — R-phase CT reversed + B-phase PT fuse missing

Combined fault. Element 1 reverses, Element 2 loses V_B. Registration = near zero or negative. Severe under-registration.

Case 10 — Both PT fuses missing (both voltage circuits open)

Both elements lose voltage. Meter does not register. Zero registration. Immediately apparent — no reading.

Case 11 — Current circuit R&B interchanged + R CT reversed

Compound fault. Complex phasor result. Approximately –86.6% at unity PF (meter reverses at high rate).

Case 12 — All CT polarities reversed + one PT fuse open

Maximum under-registration. Net registration = –50%. Both disc reversal and element loss combine.
3-Phase 4-Wire Meter — 16 Fault Cases▼

Normal registration for 3φ 4-wire 3-element meter = 3·V·I·cos φ. Each element independent. Neutral is reference.

Case 1 — One CT Open (R-phase current missing)

2 of 3 elements active. Registration = 66.7%. Meter under-reads by 1/3.

Case 2 — One CT Polarity Reversed (R-phase)

Element 1 reverses. Net = 2 forward – 1 reverse = 33.3% at unity PF. Severe under-registration.

Case 3 — Two CTs Polarity Reversed (R & Y reversed)

1 forward – 2 reverse. Registration = –33.3% (meter reverses slowly). Energy credited to consumer.

Case 4 — All Three CTs Polarity Reversed

All elements reverse. Registration = –100%. Full reverse rotation. Classic tamper scenario.

Case 5 — One Phase PT Fuse Open (R-phase voltage missing)

Element 1 loses voltage, reads zero. Elements 2&3 correct. Registration = 66.7%.

Case 6 — Two Phase PT Fuses Open (R & Y missing)

Only B-phase element active. Registration = 33.3%.

Case 7 — All PT Fuses Open

No voltage reference. Zero registration. All elements dead. Easily detected by inspection.

Case 8 — R & Y Phase Current Leads Interchanged

Elements 1&2 see wrong phase currents — phasor displacement. Registration ≈ 66.7% · cos(60°) ≈ 33% at unity PF. Significant under-read.

Case 9 — R & B Phase Current Leads Interchanged

Similar phasor displacement but different angle. Registration ≈ 50% at unity PF.

Case 10 — Neutral (N) current wire missing from meter

Voltage reference lost for all elements. If internally connected to neutral bus, may affect LT direct meter significantly. Registration = 0 to 100% depending on balance.

Case 11 — One CT reversed + One PT fuse open (same phase)

That element contributes nothing (no V) or negative (reversed I). Net = 66.7% (other two elements correct).

Case 12 — One CT reversed + One PT fuse open (different phases)

Two elements affected independently. One reverses, one drops to zero. Net = 33.3% from single correct element.

Case 13 — R-phase CT reversed + Y-phase CT open

Element 1: negative. Element 2: zero. Element 3: correct. Net = 0% if 1 and 3 cancel, or small positive.

Case 14 — Phase sequence reversed (R-Y-B becomes R-B-Y)

Reactive element reversed. KVARH meter may record wrong direction. KWH largely unaffected for balanced load but KVARH error causes PF penalty issue.

Case 15 — Voltage and current of different phases cross-connected

Complex phasor mismatch. Registration = cos(120°)·3·VI = –50% at unity PF for full cross-connection. Meter reverses.

Case 16 — External shunt across CT secondary

Part of CT secondary current bypassed. Meter sees reduced current. Registration proportional to (Rm / Rm+Rshunt). Under-reads by shunt ratio.
KVARH Element Vector Analysis▼

KVARH Measurement Principle

For 3-phase 3-wire reactive energy meter, voltage phasors are phase-shifted by 90° before application to current elements. Standard cross-connection method:

  • Element 1: I_R × V_YB (phase-shifted 90°) → measures Q_R component
  • Element 2: I_B × V_RY (phase-shifted 90°) → measures Q_B component
  • Total KVAR = (V_YB·I_R·sinφ + V_RY·I_B·sinφ) / √3
  • Forward registration = lagging KVAR (inductive load)
  • Reverse registration = leading KVAR (capacitive load)

Power Factor from Meter Readings

PF = cos φ = KWH / KVAH

KVAH = √(KWH² + KVARH²)

tan φ = KVARH / KWH

PF incentive threshold (APSEB): 0.90 lagging
PF penalty below: 0.85 lagging
Fault Detection Methods▼

Dial Test

Compare meter disc/pulse rate at known load. Calculate expected kWh. Compare with meter reading over timed interval. Registration % = (actual/expected) × 100.

Phantom Load Test

Apply known calibrated current/voltage from test kit at TTB. Meter runs on phantom load. Check % error against standard test meter simultaneously.

Clamp Meter Check

Measure actual current on each phase conductor. Compare with meter display current. Discrepancy indicates CT or connection fault.

Vector / Phasor Diagram

Use power analyser. Compare angle between V and I on each element. Cross-reference with expected load PF. Polarity reversal gives 180° offset.

Online Test (MRT Kit)

Portable testing kit connected to TTB while meter energised. Checks all three phase currents, voltages, phase angles, and % error simultaneously.

Consumption Pattern Analysis

Compare month-on-month consumption. Sudden unexplained drop indicates fault/tampering. Load survey comparison vs sanctioned MD.

Field Testing Procedures

Phantom load test, dial test, online test, MRT lab, sealing

Phantom Load Test▼

Test without disturbing consumer load. Current injected from portable source via TTB. Voltage from PT secondary or external source.

Equipment Required

  • Portable current source (0–5A, or 0–1A variable)
  • Burden resistors / inductors (adjustable PF)
  • Standard reference meter (class 0.1 or 0.2)
  • Stopwatch / revolution counter
  • TTB access key and shorting tools

Test Procedure

1

Short CT secondaries at TTB

Isolate meter CT circuit using TTB shorting links.

2

Inject phantom current

Apply calibrated test current (typically 5A) at test current terminal of TTB.

3

Apply voltage

Use PT secondary voltage (live) or portable voltage source at test voltage terminal.

4

Count revolutions / pulses

Time N revolutions of disc (or count N optical pulses for static meter). Compare with reference meter.

5

Calculate % error

% error = [(Wm – Ws)/Ws] × 100. Acceptable: ±2% (class 1), ±1% (class 0.5).

% Error = (W_meter – W_standard) / W_standard × 100
KWH per revolution = 1000 / (MF × disc_constant)
Dial / Disc Test (On-Load)▼

Method — Timed Disc Revolutions

  • Note meter constant K (rev/kWh) on nameplate
  • Note CT ratio, PT ratio → calculate MF
  • Measure actual current on phase conductors (clamp meter)
  • Note voltmeter reading and power factor (if meter displays)
  • Calculate expected kWh: W_exp = V × I × cosφ × t / 1000
  • Count N revolutions, time T seconds
  • Calculate actual kWh: W_act = N / (K × MF)
  • Compare W_act vs W_exp
Time for N revolutions:
T_expected = N × 3600 × 1000 / (P × K × MF)

where P = actual load in Watts

% Error = (T_expected – T_actual) / T_expected × 100
Acceptable error limits:
Induction disc: ±2% class 1, ±1% class 0.5
Static meter: ±1% class 1, ±0.5% class 0.5S
Online Test (MRT — Meter Reading Test)▼

MRT (Meter Reading Test) kit is a portable power analyser connected to TTB test terminals while meter energised. Performs complete electrical testing without interrupting supply.

What MRT Measures

  • Phase voltages (V_R, V_Y, V_B)
  • Phase currents (I_R, I_Y, I_B)
  • Phase angles (φ_R, φ_Y, φ_B)
  • Active power per phase
  • % error of meter under test

Connection at TTB

  • Current: clamp-on CT or series injection at A terminals
  • Voltage: from L (link) terminals
  • Optical sensor or pulse input from meter
  • No need to short CT or isolate voltage
  • Non-intrusive for most modern kits

RSS Testing Kit Panel

  • Rack-mounted or portable panel
  • Used in laboratory (MRT lab at division)
  • Can test multiple meters in sequence
  • Generates test report with % error at various load points (1%, 5%, 25%, 50%, 100%, 120% of rated current)
MRT Lab Requirement (APSEB): Each meter must be tested at MRT laboratory before installation. Test report attached to consumer file. Meters failing ±2% limit rejected.
Earth Resistance Measurement▼

Equipment: EVERSHED Megger (Earth Tester)

  • 3-terminal earth tester (most accurate method)
  • Terminals: E (Earth under test), P (Potential), C (Current)
  • 2-terminal method: for quick check (includes lead resistance)
  • Operating frequency: 128 Hz or similar (avoids DC and 50Hz interference)

3-Terminal Method (Fall-of-Potential)

1

Drive auxiliary electrodes

C electrode: 30m from E. P electrode: 20m from E (62% of 30m distance).

2

Connect megger

E→ earth electrode, P→ potential spike, C→ current spike.

3

Read resistance

Rotate handle at 160 rpm. Read stable value on ohm scale.

4

Verify with ±20% P movement

Move P ±20% of CE distance. If reading stable: valid result. If varies: widen electrode spacing.

Phase Sequence Indicator▼

Operation

Small device with 3 terminals (R, Y, B) and neon/LED indicators. Rotating disc indicates phase rotation direction.

  • Correct sequence R-Y-B: forward rotation (clockwise disc or right LED lights)
  • Wrong sequence R-B-Y: reverse rotation (anticlockwise or left LED)
  • Used before CT/PT connection to verify phase sequence matches meter terminal labelling
  • Essential before commissioning HT consumers with motors
Why Phase Sequence Matters for Meters:
KVARH element measures reactive power direction based on assumed phase sequence. Wrong sequence → KVARH registers in wrong direction → power factor calculation error → billing error on two-part tariff.
Standard APSEB: R-Y-B sequence mandatory. Verify with indicator before sealing meter. Record in inspection report.

Phase Shifting Transformer (MC-21 / GE) — ABT Application▼

Phase Shifting Transformer (PST)

Controls active power flow between grids by introducing phase angle difference between sending and receiving end voltages.

  • Used at 400kV interconnection points
  • Model MC-21: manufactured by GE
  • Introduces phase advance or retard (±15° typical)
  • Active power flow: P = (V1·V2·sinδ) / X
  • Increasing δ increases power transfer
P = V₁·V₂ · sin(δ) / X
PST shifts δ → controls P flow

Metering of PST

  • ABT meter on both primary and secondary of PST
  • UI (Unscheduled Interchange) measured at injection point
  • Frequency deviation from 50Hz → UI meter records deviation energy
  • Above 50Hz: generator over-injecting → UI credit
  • Below 50Hz: generator under-injecting → UI charge
ABT Frequency Zones (CERC):
49.2–50.0 Hz: UI charges apply (increasing with deviation)
50.0–50.5 Hz: UI credit for generators
<49.0 Hz: Emergency — load shedding protocol
Transformer Vector Groups — DYn11▼

Standard Distribution Transformer: DYn11

  • D = Delta connected primary (11kV side)
  • Y = Star connected secondary (440V/415V side)
  • n = Neutral brought out on secondary
  • 11 = Secondary lags primary by 30° (11 × 30° = 330° = –30° lag)
Clock notation: 11 = –30° or 330° lag
V_secondary lags V_primary by 30°
Why DYn11 is standard: Delta primary suppresses 3rd harmonic circulation. Star secondary with neutral allows single-phase loads. 30° phase shift breaks circulating current in parallel transformers.

Vector Group Impact on Metering

  • CT on delta side: measures line current (= phase current in delta)
  • CT on star side: measures line current (= phase current in star)
  • 30° phase shift means PT and CT phasors for differential protection must be corrected in relay software
  • For energy metering: phase shift irrelevant — same power both sides (minus losses)
  • DYn1 transformer: secondary leads by 30° (rare in India)

Buchholz Relay & Transformer Protection▼

Buchholz Relay

Gas-actuated relay installed in pipe between transformer main tank and conservator tank. Detects internal faults.

  • Float-operated mercury switch (upper float) → alarm on slow gas accumulation
  • Surge flap (lower element) → trip on heavy gas surge from severe fault
  • Gas accumulation indicates: insulation breakdown, overheating, core fault, arcing
  • Gas sample extracted from valve — tested: CO2 (insulation), H2 (oil breakdown), C2H2 (arcing)

Other Transformer Protections

  • Differential protection (87T): Compares primary and secondary currents. Operates on difference (fault current). Requires vector group correction in relay.
  • Restricted Earth Fault (64R/87N): Detects earth fault near neutral of star winding.
  • Overcurrent (51/50): Backup protection for through faults.
  • Temperature protection: Oil temp (OTI) and winding temp (WTI) relays. Alarm at 85°C, trip at 100°C (typical).
  • Pressure Relief Device (PRD): Rupture disk for sudden over-pressure. Trip auxiliary contact.
