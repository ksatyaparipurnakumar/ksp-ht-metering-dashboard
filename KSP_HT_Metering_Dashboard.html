CT Polarity — DC Kick Test

Connect a 1.5V battery between P1 and P2. Observe the galvanometer on S1-S2. On make: pointer deflects positively → polarity correct (P1→S1 subtractive). On reverse: use S2 as reference. Essential before commissioning any metering CT.

CT Burden

Total impedance (VA or Ω) connected to the CT secondary. Exceeding rated burden increases ratio error, compromising accuracy class. Standard secondaries: 1A (long distances) and 5A (local panels). Typical metering burden: 5–15 VA.

Electromagnetic PT vs CVT (Capacitor Voltage Transformer)

+

Electromagnetic PT

Standard wound design. Suitable up to 220kV. High accuracy across all burdens. Used where accuracy Class 0.2 is required. More susceptible to ferro-resonance at EHV levels.

Vout = Vin × (N₂/N₁)
Error <0.2% at rated burden

CVT — Capacitor Voltage Transformer

Preferred at 220kV and above. Uses a capacitor voltage divider in series with an electromagnetic unit. Lower cost at EHV, provides carrier coupling for PLC (power line communication). Accuracy slightly lower at light burden — Class 0.5 typical.

Vc = Vin × C₁/(C₁+C₂)
Electromagnetic stage → 110V output
ℹ
5-limb PT core design is required for obtaining zero-sequence voltage (3V₀) needed for earth fault relay operation. A 3-limb design cannot support zero-sequence flux and thus cannot measure residual voltage.

Reactive Power (kVAR) Measurement — Cross-Phase Connection

To measure reactive power, a cross-phase connection is used. The B-phase current is measured against the R-phase voltage (90° displaced), effectively measuring sin φ instead of cos φ. This gives kVAr directly from the meter element:

W_reactive = I_B × V_R × sin(φ)   [Cross-phase method for 3-wire system]
kVAR = kW × tan(φ)            [from power triangle]
kVA² = kW² + kVAR²           [apparent power]

Commissioning & Testing Procedures

Systematic procedures for pre-commissioning tests, Test Terminal Block (TTB) operation, and phantom load testing of HT metering installations.

Pre-Commissioning Tests

Mandatory tests before energising any new HT metering installation:

  • Insulation Resistance (IR) Test: Megger test at 1kV DC. CT secondary to earth: >1MΩ. PT secondary to earth: >1MΩ. All wiring IR: >1MΩ.
  • CT Polarity Check: DC kick test on each CT. Verify S1 terminal deflects +ve when current enters P1. Confirm all CTs have same polarity orientation.
  • CT Ratio Test: Inject primary current, measure secondary. Verify ratio within ±0.1% of nameplate. Use ratio test set or inject at reduced current.
  • PT Ratio & Polarity: Apply LV to primary (e.g., 240V AC). Measure secondary — verify ratio. Check polarity markings (H1=X1 subtractive).
  • Continuity Check: All CT-to-meter and PT-to-meter wiring continuity verified with buzzer/ohmmeter.
  • Burden Measurement: Measure actual burden on each CT secondary. Must be within nameplate burden to maintain accuracy class.
  • Earth Continuity: All CT/PT secondary earths connected and verified. Only one earth point per CT secondary.

Test Terminal Block (TTB) — Operation

The TTB allows meter isolation for testing without de-energising the primary circuit.

⚠
ALWAYS close CT shorting links BEFORE disconnecting the energy meter from the TTB. Failure to do so causes CT open-circuit — a dangerous and potentially lethal condition.

Phantom Load Testing

Testing a meter at full primary load (e.g., 100A at 11kV) is wasteful and impractical. A "Phantom Load" test bench simulates actual load conditions using low-power test equipment.

How Phantom Load Works

  • Current injection unit supplies rated CT secondary current (e.g., 5A) to meter current coil
  • Voltage injection unit supplies rated PT secondary voltage (e.g., 110V) to meter voltage coil
  • Phase angle between injected V and I is controlled precisely (e.g., 0° for unity PF, 30° for 0.866 PF)
  • Meter pulse output is counted over a timed period and compared against theoretical energy
  • Meter accuracy (% error) is calculated. Acceptable limit: ±0.5% for Class 0.5S meters
Theoretical Energy = V × I × cos(φ) × t

% Error = [(Emeasured - Etheoretical) / Etheoretical] × 100

Test current: rated (100%) & light load (10%)
Test PF: 1.0 (unity) and 0.5 (lagging)
ℹ
Phantom load testing does NOT consume actual load power. The current and voltage circuits are electrically isolated and powered by small test sources.

Phasor analysis of HT metering connections and systematic case studies showing how wiring errors affect energy registration. Based on the standard torque equation: W = V·I·cos φ.

Standard Torque Equation: T = K · V · I · cos(φ)
2-Element: W_total = V_RB · I_R · cos(φ₁) + V_YB · I_Y · cos(φ₂)
Normal registration at Unity PF: 100% energy recorded
Error %: [(Actual Reading − True Reading) / True Reading] × 100

Case 1 — Normal Connection (100% Registration)

W = VR·IR·cosφ + VY·IY·cosφ = 100% at UPF

Case 3 — CT Polarity Reversed (One Phase)

Phasor IR flips 180°
W₁ = VR·(-IR)·cosφ → opposes W₂
At UPF: ~57% registration (varies with φ)

Case 2 — CT Secondary Open (R-Phase)

IR = 0 → W1 = VR·0·cosφ = 0
Wtotal = 0 + VBY·IB·cosφ ≈ 50%

Case 4 — R & B Phase Currents Interchanged

W = VR·IB·cos(120°−φ) + VB·IR·cos(120°+φ)
At UPF: W ≈ −50% to 0% — meter may run reverse

Case 5 — PT Fuse Blown (R-Phase)

VR = 0 → W1 = 0·IR·cosφ = 0
4-wire: Wtotal ≈ 2/3 × normal = ~67%

Transformer Vector Groups — HT Applications

Dyn11

Most common distribution transformer. Delta (HV) / Star-grounded (LV). LV leads HV by 30° (11 o'clock position). Delta HV blocks 3rd harmonic. Star LV provides neutral for single-phase loads. Used: 33/0.4kV, 11/0.4kV distribution.

YNd11

Star-grounded (HV) / Delta (LV). Used in step-up applications (generating station). Neutral earthing on HV side. LV Delta winding handles circulating harmonic currents without sending them into the system.

YNa0

Auto-transformer with zero phase displacement (a0 = 0 o'clock). Used for voltage regulation without phase shift. Common at bulk supply points: 132/33kV, 33/11kV. Economical for voltage ratios close to 1:1.

ℹ
Why Delta/Star? The Delta HV winding blocks 3rd harmonic currents from entering the network. The Star LV winding with a neutral provides a path for single-phase loads and earth fault current. This combination is standard for all distribution transformers in India (IS 2026).

Chapter 1 — Introduction to High Tension Metering

High Tension (HT) metering refers to the precise measurement of electrical energy consumed at voltages above 1 kV. In India, consumers drawing power at 11 kV and above are classified as HT consumers, while Extra High Voltage (EHV) consumers operate at 110 kV and above. Revenue metering at these voltage levels demands instrumentation-grade accuracy, specialised safety provisions, and compliance with national and international standards.

Why HT Metering is Different

Direct connection of measuring instruments to HV lines is neither safe nor practical. Instead, Instrument Transformers — Current Transformers (CTs) and Potential/Voltage Transformers (PTs/VTs) — step down the primary current and voltage to safe, standardised secondary levels: 5 A (or 1 A) for CTs and 110 V (or 63.5 V) for PTs. The meters and protection equipment are then connected to these secondary circuits.

Blondel's Theorem

Blondel's theorem states that for an N-wire polyphase system, complete power measurement requires N−1 measuring elements (wattmeters). This fundamental principle governs the design of all HT metering schemes:

  • 3-wire Delta system → 2 elements (2-element metering). The third winding's contribution is inferred mathematically.
  • 4-wire Star system → 3 elements (3-element metering). Each phase is independently measured.
ℹ
The 2-element scheme is only valid when the system is balanced or the third wire has no current (no neutral). If the neutral carries current (e.g., unbalanced single-phase loads), the 3-element scheme is mandatory. → HT Supply Standards

Accuracy Standards

Revenue metering accuracy is governed by IS 13779 (tri-vector meters) and IS 14697 (CTs for metering). Key accuracy classes:

  • Class 0.2S / 0.5S — Revenue metering CTs (special application, wider current range: 1%–120% rated)
  • Class 0.5 / 1.0 — General purpose metering
  • Class 0.1 — Laboratory / precision reference standard
  • Class 3P / 5P — Protection class (not suitable for billing)

Chapter 2 — Electrical Symbols & ASDN Relay Codes

A unified graphical language is essential for interpreting HT schematic drawings, protection diagrams, and metering cubicle wiring. The symbols below follow IS 11049 / IEC 60617 and the ANSI/IEEE C37.2 standard for device function numbers.

Primary Apparatus Symbols

  • OCB (Oil Circuit Breaker) — A circle with a diagonal bar inside, line entry/exit top and bottom. Used up to 33 kV.
  • VCB (Vacuum Circuit Breaker) — Similar symbol with "V" annotation. Used 11–33 kV; compact, low maintenance.
  • SF6 CB — Annotated "SF6"; used at 66 kV and above for gas-insulated switchgear (GIS).
  • DS (Disconnector / Isolator) — Two parallel bars with a gap and blade. No interruption capability; isolates only when circuit is dead.
  • ES (Earth Switch) — Disconnector symbol with grounding symbol at the blade tip. Earths the isolated section for safe working.
  • LA (Lightning Arrester) — Zigzag within a circle. Connected between line and earth to clamp switching/lightning surges.

Instrument Transformer Symbols

  • CT — Two overlapping circles on the primary conductor. Dashed lines indicate the secondary circuit. The "×" mark on one terminal indicates the primary P1/S1 polarity dot.
  • PT / VT — Two overlapping ellipses, typically shown as a two-winding transformer connected between line and earth.
  • CVT — A capacitor divider in series with a VT, used above 110 kV for economic reasons.

Chapter 3 — Current Transformers

The Current Transformer (CT) is the most critical element in HT metering. It steps down the primary current (e.g., 200 A, 1000 A) to a standardised secondary level (5 A or 1 A) that can be safely connected to meters, relays, and recorders.

Construction Types

  • Bar Primary CT — The busbar or cable itself serves as the single-turn primary. Simple, used for high-current applications (above 800 A).
  • Wound Primary CT — Multiple turns of primary conductor wound on the core. Used for low-current ratios (up to ~200 A). Better accuracy.
  • Ring CT (Toroidal) — Circular core through which the primary conductor passes. Compact, used for cable installations and earth fault detection.
  • Cascade CT — Used at EHV (220 kV, 400 kV). Multiple cores stacked in series to achieve very high voltage isolation.

CT Equivalent Circuit & Error Sources

A CT is not a perfect current source. The excitation branch (magnetising impedance) draws a small magnetising current Iₑ from the primary, causing two types of error:

  • Ratio Error (Current Error) — The secondary current does not exactly equal (Primary Current ÷ Turns Ratio). Formula: Ratio Error % = [(kₙIₛ − I₁)/I₁] × 100
  • Phase Displacement Error — A small angular shift (in minutes) between the primary and reversed secondary current phasors. Critical for power factor measurement.
Ratio Error % = (kₙ × Iₛ − I₁) / I₁ × 100
Phase Error (minutes) ≈ (Iₑ × sin δ / I₁) × 3438

Accuracy Class and Burden

The accuracy class (0.1, 0.2, 0.2S, 0.5, 1, 3, 5) defines the maximum permissible ratio error (as a percentage) and phase error (in minutes). The class is maintained only when the connected burden (total impedance of meters, relays, and wiring resistance) is within the rated VA burden.

🔴
Critical Safety Rule: A CT secondary circuit must NEVER be left open-circuited while primary current is flowing. Without a closed secondary circuit, the primary current magnetises the core to saturation, generating a dangerously high voltage (several kilovolts) at the open terminals. Always short-circuit the secondary terminals before opening any series connection. → CT Theory Deep-Dive

CT Polarity (Dot Convention)

CT polarity markings ensure that the phase relationship between primary and secondary is maintained correctly for directional protection and power measurement. The convention is:

  • P1 / S1 — Paired primary and secondary terminals (instantaneous current enters P1, exits S1)
  • P2 / S2 — The other paired terminals
  • In schematics, a dot or asterisk (×) marks the P1/S1 (positive) polarity terminals

A reversed CT polarity (P1 connected where P2 should be, or S1 and S2 interchanged) causes the current phasor to rotate 180°, appearing as leading power factor when the load is actually lagging, and can cause the unit to under-register by nearly 200% of true consumption.

CT Testing — Polarity & Insulation

  • Polarity test: Apply a 6V DC battery across P1-P2. A momentary positive deflection on a galvanometer at S1-S2 confirms correct polarity (P1 ↔ S1).
  • Insulation test: Megger at 2.5 kV DC between primary and earth, and secondary and earth. Should be >100 MΩ. → MRT Standards
  • Magnetisation curve (knee-point test): Apply increasing voltage to secondary with primary open; the voltage at which the excitation current increases sharply is the knee-point voltage (Vk). Protection CTs require Vk ≥ 2× (RCT + RL) × ISC_max.

Chapter 4 — Voltage / Potential Transformers

Voltage Transformers (VTs) or Potential Transformers (PTs) reduce the HV bus voltage to a safe secondary level of 110 V (line-to-line, for Delta-connected PTs) or 63.5 V (phase-to-neutral, for Star-connected PTs) for use by meters, protection relays, and synchronising equipment.

Types of VTs

  • Electromagnetic VT — Wound-core transformer. Accurate from 50 Hz systems. Limited to below 66 kV due to insulation costs.
  • Capacitor Voltage Transformer (CVT) — Uses a capacitor divider to step down the voltage before the electromagnetic stage. Economical above 110 kV. Has a small phase error at frequencies other than 50 Hz.

PT Connection Configurations

  • Single-phase PT: Connected between two phases or between one phase and neutral. Gives phase-to-phase or phase-to-neutral voltage.
  • V-V (Open Delta): Two single-phase PTs connected in open delta. Provides all three line voltages using only two units. Cost-saving but no neutral reference.
  • Star/Star (YN/yn): Three PTs connected in star on both primary and secondary. Provides both phase voltages and line voltages, including neutral reference for earth fault detection (zero-sequence voltage detection).
  • Star/Open Delta (YN/d): Secondary open-delta connected across a voltage relay or earthing-fault device. Zero-sequence (residual) voltage appears across the open-delta terminals during earth fault — used for earth fault detection on unearthed systems.

PT Burden and Accuracy

The PT accuracy class (0.1, 0.2, 0.5, 1, 3) defines the maximum voltage error and phase displacement, maintained when the total connected burden (in VA) is within the rated burden. Excess burden degrades accuracy; insufficient burden is generally acceptable.

⚠
PT safety: Unlike CTs, VT secondaries must NEVER be short-circuited. A secondary short-circuit draws excessive current from the primary, can damage the VT winding and fuses, and removes the voltage signal from all connected relays. Always connect in open-circuit (voltage measurement mode). → PT/VT Deep-Dive

Ferroresonance

Ferroresonance is a rare but potentially damaging phenomenon in VT circuits. It occurs when the non-linear (saturating) inductance of the VT resonates with the capacitance of the bus or cable at a frequency that may be a sub-harmonic (e.g., 16.67 Hz or 25 Hz) of the supply frequency. Symptoms include sustained overvoltages and abnormal VT heating. It is suppressed by connecting a resistive load (typically 40–200 Ω) across the VT secondary or tertiary winding (also called "damping resistor" or "anti-ferroresonance resistor").

Chapter 5 — HT Metering Architectures

The metering cubicle contains the secondary circuits of the instrument transformers connected to the tri-vector energy meter (TVS meter), ammeters, voltmeters, and protection relays. The architecture depends on the system configuration and accuracy requirements.

2-Element Metering (3-Wire Delta System)

Used for 3-phase, 3-wire (Delta) systems. Based on Blondel's theorem: only 2 current measurements are needed. Typically, CTs in R and B phases; the Y-phase current is algebraically the negative sum of R and B and need not be measured.

  • Current elements: CT on R-phase (element 1) and CT on B-phase (element 2)
  • Voltage elements: R-Y voltage for element 1, B-Y voltage for element 2
  • The two elements together give correct total 3-phase power for any balanced or unbalanced load, provided no neutral current flows
P_total = V_RY × I_R × cos(angle) + V_BY × I_B × cos(angle)

3-Element Metering (4-Wire Star System)

Used for 3-phase, 4-wire (Star) systems where neutral current may flow (e.g., unbalanced single-phase loads, mixed loads). All three phases are independently measured.

  • CTs in R, Y, and B phases
  • Voltage: R-N, Y-N, B-N (phase-to-neutral)
  • Three independent metering elements
  • Mandatory when the neutral current is non-zero

Metering Cubicle Layout

The metering cubicle is a dedicated compartment (or panel) that houses:

  • Test Terminal Block (TTB) — Provides access for testing and inspection of CT/PT circuits without disturbing the primary circuit or the meter
  • Tri-Vector Energy Meter (TVS Meter) — Measures kWh, kVARh (lag and lead), kVAh, and power factor simultaneously
  • Maximum Demand Indicator (MDI) — Records the highest power demand in a defined interval (15 or 30 minutes)
  • Ammeters and Voltmeters — For operational monitoring (not billing)
  • Selector Switches — For ammeter phase selection and voltmeter phase selection

Test Terminal Block (TTB) Operation

The TTB is the most important diagnostic access point in the metering secondary circuit. It has a dedicated link arrangement that allows:

  • Short-circuit the CT secondary (essential before opening the CT circuit)
  • Disconnect the CT from the meter (for meter replacement without de-energising the primary)
  • Inject test current from a phantom load tester to check meter accuracy in-service
  • Verify polarity and phase connections using a phase angle meter
🔴
TTB Sequence: When disconnecting a CT from the meter: (1) First short-circuit the CT secondary at the TTB, THEN (2) open the circuit to the meter. Never reverse the order — opening first, then shorting, leaves the CT open-circuited during the interval between the two actions.

Chapter 6 — Commissioning & Testing Procedures

Before a metering installation is put into service, a systematic series of tests must be performed and documented. These tests verify the correctness of wiring, the integrity of insulation, and the accuracy of the metering system.

Pre-Commissioning Tests

1. Visual Inspection

Check CT and PT terminal markings (P1, P2, S1, S2), verify wiring against the approved schematic, inspect for loose connections, damage, and correct earthing of CT and PT secondary neutrals. Verify TTB link positions are correctly set.

2. Insulation Resistance (IR) Test

Using a 500 V or 2.5 kV Megger:

  • CT primary to earth: >100 MΩ
  • CT secondary to earth: >50 MΩ
  • PT primary to secondary: >100 MΩ
  • Meter terminals to case/earth: >20 MΩ
  • Wiring to earth (all secondary): >1 MΩ minimum

3. CT Polarity Test

Apply 6V DC battery positive to P1, negative to P2. Observe momentary kick on galvanometer at S1-S2: positive deflection confirms correct polarity (P1↔S1). Reverse deflection indicates reversed secondary connection — swap S1 and S2. → Full MRT Lab Standards

4. Phase Angle and Phasor Verification

Using a Phase Angle Meter (PAM) or Power Factor Meter connected at the TTB with primary energised:

  • Verify R-phase current lags R-phase voltage by the expected load power factor angle
  • Verify phase sequence: R, Y, B (anticlockwise rotation in standard phasor diagram)
  • Check that each element of the tri-vector meter sees the correct V and I combination

5. Phantom Load Test + Creep & Starting Current Tests

A phantom load tester injects a known, calibrated current at a known power factor through the TTB while the primary is energised. The meter reading is compared against the injected reference. The error in % is:

Error % = (Meter Reading − Reference Reading) / Reference Reading × 100
Allowable error: ±2% for Class 0.5S meters, ±1% for Class 0.2S

Chapter 7 — Vector Analysis & Phasor Diagrams

Phasor diagrams are the primary diagnostic tool for verifying correct metering and detecting wiring errors. Understanding the expected phasor relationships for various load types is essential for every commissioning engineer.

Reference Frame Convention

By convention, the R-phase voltage V_R is placed along the positive X-axis (0°, pointing right). The three phase voltages are 120° apart in anticlockwise sequence: V_R (0°), V_Y (−120° or 240°), V_B (−240° or 120°). Line voltages: V_RY = V_R − V_Y, leading V_R by 30°.

Normal Load Phasor (Lagging PF)

For a typical inductive load (motors, transformers), the current I lags the voltage V by angle φ. In the phasor diagram:

  • V_R: at 0° (reference)
  • I_R: at −φ (below V_R by angle φ)
  • This places the operating point in Quadrant 1 (+P, +Q) — importing active and reactive power
  • The meter should register positive kWh and positive kVARh (lag)

Capacitive Load Phasor (Leading PF)

For capacitive loads or over-excited synchronous motors:

  • I_R leads V_R by angle φ (positive φ means leading)
  • Operating point in Quadrant 4 (+P, −Q) — importing active, exporting reactive
  • Meter registers positive kWh and positive kVARh (lead)

Diagnosing CT Reversal from Phasor Diagram → Fault Diagnosis Table | Full Vector Reference

A reversed CT secondary (S1↔S2 swapped) causes I_R to shift by exactly 180°. Instead of the current appearing at −30° (lagging PF = 0.866), it would appear at +150°. This moves the operating point from Q1 to Q2, and the power factor indicator reads leading (or even negative power factor) for a known inductive load. The power registration may be negative or severely reduced.

ℹ
Rule of thumb: If the power factor reading seems wrong for the known load type (e.g., reading 0.3 leading for a motor load), always check CT polarity first. A 180° current reversal on even one phase can produce dramatic errors in the combined three-phase power reading.

Chapter 8 — Protection Relaying for HT Installations

Protection relays safeguard the HT installation against abnormal electrical conditions. Each relay is assigned an ANSI/IEEE device function number. The following are the most commonly encountered relays in HT consumer installations.

Overcurrent and Earth Fault Protection

  • 51 IDMT Relay — Inverse Definite Minimum Time relay. Operating time is inversely proportional to the magnitude of the fault current. Uses Standard Inverse (SI), Very Inverse (VI), or Extremely Inverse (EI) time-current characteristic curve.
  • 50 High-Set Element — Instantaneous element set above the maximum through-fault current. Trips in <80 ms for solid short-circuits.
  • 51N Ground Fault — Measures the residual (zero-sequence) current: I_R + I_Y + I_B = 3I₀. Set at 10–20% of rated current. Detects single-phase-to-earth faults.

Differential Protection (87T)

The differential relay (87T) compares the current entering the transformer on the HV side with the current leaving on the LV side. Under normal load or through-fault conditions, the difference is approximately zero. Under an internal fault (winding-to-winding or winding-to-earth), the difference becomes large, and the relay trips.

Key challenges in 87T implementation:

  • CT ratio mismatch: HV and LV CTs have different ratios; compensated in modern numerical relays by software ratio correction
  • Vector group compensation: For Dyn11 transformers, there is a 30° phase shift between HV and LV; compensated by connecting HV CTs in delta and LV CTs in star (or via software in numerical relays)
  • Magnetising inrush restraint: At energisation, a large 2nd harmonic inrush current flows; the relay must not trip for this. Modern relays use 2nd harmonic restraint (typically 20% of fundamental) to distinguish inrush from fault.

Buchholz Relay

The Buchholz relay is a gas-operated protection device installed in the pipe between the conservator and the main tank of oil-filled transformers. It provides two levels of protection:

  • Alarm stage (upper float): Slow accumulation of gas (from minor insulation decomposition) lifts the upper float, closing the alarm contact. The trapped gas can be sampled and tested to identify the fault type by dissolved gas analysis (DGA).
  • Trip stage (lower float / surge plate): A sudden surge of oil (from a major internal fault with rapid gas generation) strikes the lower float or surge flap, tripping the main circuit breaker immediately.

Chapter 9 — Special Applications

Traction Metering (Railway Electrification)

Indian Railways electrification uses a single-phase 25 kV, 50 Hz system. Traction substations tap one phase at a time from the 3-phase grid, creating inherent phase imbalance. The metering scheme must handle this single-phase loading with precision:

  • 1-element metering with a single CT and a single PT
  • Load is typically highly variable and regenerative (returned energy from regenerative braking must also be metered)
  • 4-quadrant metering is essential to capture both imported and exported energy
  • Scott-T transformer may be used at some substations to convert 3-phase to two single-phase supplies for balanced loading

Earthing & Grounding Systems

Earthing resistance standards (as per IE Rules and IS 3043):

  • Large grid substations (132 kV+): ≤ 0.5 Ω
  • HT consumer premises: ≤ 2 Ω
  • Distribution transformers: ≤ 5 Ω
  • LT consumers: ≤ 10 Ω

The Fall of Potential (3-point) method using an earth megger ("Evershed") is the standard measurement technique. Step potential (<50 V RMS) and touch potential (<50 V RMS) limits must be verified for HV substations.

ABT (Availability-Based Tariff) Metering

ABT is a frequency-linked tariff mechanism for bulk power generators and state utilities connected to the Indian national grid. → HT Supply Voltage Standards | 4-Quadrant Energy

  • Special Energy Meters (SEM) with 0.2S class accuracy
  • Metering at the inter-utility boundary point
  • Time-block resolution of 15 minutes (consumers >1500 kVA)
  • Tamper-proof time synchronisation (GPS or NTP)
  • 4-quadrant energy registration: captures both import/export of active and reactive energy
  • Minimum power factor 0.85 monthly average — penalties apply below this threshold
ℹ
MRT Lab Standards: CT burden 15 VA, PT burden 100 VA, permissible error ±2.5%, no creep at 110% voltage. Three mandatory sealing points on every installation.

Why HT Metering?

Direct connection of instruments to high-voltage lines is unsafe and impractical. Instrument transformers bridge the gap.

  • HT consumers: 11 kV and above (India classification)
  • Primary line: thousands of volts, hundreds of amps
  • CT steps current down to safe 5 A or 1 A
  • PT steps voltage down to safe 110 V or 63.5 V
  • Enables use of standard meter, relays, recorders
  • Accuracy class maintained: 0.2S / 0.5S for billing
  • Blondel's theorem: N−1 elements for N-wire system
2 / 10

Current Transformer — CT

Steps down primary current to 5 A or 1 A. The most critical element in HT metering.

  • Never open-circuit a CT secondary under load — kilovolt surge danger
  • Accuracy class 0.2S / 0.5S for revenue metering
  • Polarity: P1↔S1 (instantaneous entry/exit)
  • Ratio error: (kₙ·Iₛ − I₁) / I₁ × 100 %
  • Phase error in minutes: from magnetising branch
  • Burden rating (VA): must not be exceeded
  • Knee-point test confirms protection CT suitability
Rated Secondary Current = Primary Current / Turns Ratio
e.g., 200/5 A CT → ratio = 40
3 / 10

Voltage / Potential Transformer

Steps down HV to 110 V (L-L) or 63.5 V (L-N) for meters and protection relays. Never short-circuit the secondary.

  • Electromagnetic PT: up to 66 kV; wound transformer
  • CVT: above 110 kV; capacitor divider + electromagnetic stage
  • V-V connection: 2 PTs give all 3 line voltages
  • Star/Open-delta: detects zero-sequence voltage (earth faults)
  • Ferroresonance risk: suppressed by anti-resonance resistor
  • Accuracy class 0.2 / 0.5 for revenue metering
  • Secondary must remain open-circuit (never short)
4 / 10

2-Element Metering Scheme

For 3-phase, 3-wire (Delta) systems. Blondel: only 2 of 3 phases need measurement. CTs on R and B phases.

  • CTs: R-phase and B-phase (Y-phase inferred)
  • Voltages: V_RY for element 1, V_BY for element 2
  • Correct for any balanced or unbalanced 3-wire load
  • Mathematically: P = V_RY·I_R·cos(30°−φ) + V_BY·I_B·cos(30°+φ)
  • NOT valid if neutral wire carries current
  • Used for: 11 kV industrial consumers, Delta-connected loads
  • Most common HT metering configuration in India
5 / 10

3-Element Metering Scheme

For 4-wire Star systems. All three phases independently measured when neutral current flows.

  • CTs on all 3 phases: R, Y, B
  • Voltages: V_RN, V_YN, V_BN (phase-to-neutral)
  • Mandatory when neutral current ≠ 0
  • Three independent metering elements
  • Used for: 4-wire Star consumers, mixed single/3-phase loads
  • Higher cost (3 CTs vs 2), but complete energy capture
  • Any phase imbalance is correctly metered
6 / 10

Test Terminal Block (TTB)

The TTB is the critical junction box that gives safe access to the metering CT/PT secondary circuits for in-service testing and maintenance.

  • Allows CT short-circuit before disconnecting meter
  • Enables phantom load test injection
  • Permits meter replacement without HV outage
  • Phase angle verification via PAM test
  • Sequence: Short CT first → then open meter circuit
  • Never reverse the sequence — CT open-circuit risk
  • Sealing provisions for anti-tamper (revenue protection)
7 / 10

4-Quadrant Phasor Analysis

The phasor diagram reveals the operating quadrant, power factor, and correctness of metering connections.

  • Q1 (+P, +Q): Import active + lagging reactive — standard inductive load
  • Q2 (−P, +Q): Export active + lagging reactive — inductive generator
  • Q3 (−P, −Q): Export active + leading reactive — over-excited generator
  • Q4 (+P, −Q): Import active + leading reactive — capacitor bank compensation
  • V (reference) at 0°; I at angle −φ (lagging = below V)
  • Reversed CT shifts I by 180°, moves to opposite quadrant
  • Check using Phase Angle Meter at TTB

Protection Relays — HT Systems

ANSI/IEEE device function numbers for the key protection relays in HT consumer installations and transformer protection.

  • 51 IDMT overcurrent — inverse time, feeder backup
  • 50 Instantaneous — high-set, short-circuit clearance
  • 51N Ground fault — residual current detection
  • 87T Transformer differential — primary protection
  • 64 REF relay — restricted earth fault, winding protection
  • 67 Directional overcurrent — ring mains, parallel feeders
  • Buchholz relay — internal transformer fault via gas
